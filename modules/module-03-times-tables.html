3.1 Tables 2, 5, 10 - The Easy Patterns

We start with the easiest tables to build confidence. You probably know most of these, but let's look at the patterns.

The Patterns:
  • 2s: All even numbers (ends in 0, 2, 4, 6, 8). It's just doubling!
  • 5s: Always ends in 0 or 5. (5, 10, 15, 20...)
  • 10s: Just add a zero. (10, 20, 30...)
Why This Works: Our number system is Base 10.
  • Multiply by 10 shifts digits left (adding a 0).
  • 5 is half of 10, so 5× is half of 10×. (e.g., 6×10=60, half is 30, so 6×5=30).

Worked Examples:

  1. 2 × 7: Double 7 is 14.
  2. 5 × 6: Half of 6 is 3, add 0 -> 30. (Or skip count: 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30).
  3. 10 × 4: 4 with a 0 is 40.
  4. 2 × 12: Double 12 is 24.
  5. 5 × 9: Ends in 5 because 9 is odd. 45.
Try This!
Calculate 5 × 8 using the "Half of 10" trick.
10 × 8 = 80. Half of 80 is...?
(Answer: 40)

3.2 Table 9 - The Finger Trick

The 9s table is often feared, but it's actually the easiest one thanks to a magic trick you carry with you: your hands!

The Finger Trick:
  1. Hold both hands up, palms facing you.
  2. Number fingers 1 to 10 from left to right (Left Pinky is 1, Right Pinky is 10).
  3. To do 9 × 4, fold down finger #4 (Left Index).
  4. Count fingers to the left of the folded one (3). This is the tens digit.
  5. Count fingers to the right of the folded one (6). This is the units digit.
  6. Answer: 36!
Why This Works: In the 9s table, the digits always add up to 9 (until 9x11).
  • 9×2 = 18 (1+8=9)
  • 9×3 = 27 (2+7=9)
  • The tens digit is always one less than the multiplier (e.g., for 9×7, tens is 6).
Common Mistake: Don't count the folded finger! It's the separator. The fingers to the left are tens, to the right are ones.

3.3 Tables 3, 4, 6, 8 - The Doubles Family

These tables are all related. If you know your 2s, you can get to 4s (double again) and 8s (double again!).

The Doubles Logic:
  • 4 × N: Double N, then double the result. (4 × 6 -> 12 -> 24)
  • 8 × N: Double N, double again, double again! (8 × 5 -> 10 -> 20 -> 40)
  • 6 × N: Double the 3× answer. (3 × 4 = 12, so 6 × 4 = 24)
Rhyme Time:
"6 and 8 went on a date, came back as 48." (6 × 8 = 48)
"8 and 8 fell on the floor, picked up as 64." (8 × 8 = 64)
"6 and 6 bought pick-up sticks, counted them as 36." (6 × 6 = 36)

3.4 Tables 7, 12 - The Tricky Ones

These are often the hardest for students. We use special memory hooks for 7s and a simple breakdown for 12s.

The 12s Strategy: 12 = 10 + 2.
12 × 4 = (10 × 4) + (2 × 4) = 40 + 8 = 48.
12 × 7 = (10 × 7) + (2 × 7) = 70 + 14 = 84.
7s Memory Hooks:
  • 7 × 3 = 21 (Legal drinking age in US)
  • 7 × 4 = 28 (Days in February)
  • 5, 6, 7, 8 -> 56 = 7 × 8

3.5 Table 11 - The Pattern Master

11 is magical. For single digits, you just repeat the digit (11 × 3 = 33). But did you know it works for bigger numbers too?

The 11 Trick (for 2-digit numbers):
To multiply 11 × 23:
1. Separate the 2 and 3: 2 _ 3
2. Add them (2+3=5): 2 5 3
Answer: 253!
Watch for Carries: 11 × 57
5 _ 7 -> 5+7=12. Write 2, carry 1 to the 5.
(5+1) 2 7 -> 627.
