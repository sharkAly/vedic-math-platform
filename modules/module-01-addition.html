1.1 Complement Method (Making 10s)

When you add numbers like 8 + 7, your brain has to work a little bit. But adding 10 + 5 is instant. The Complement Method teaches you to look for "friends of 10" to turn hard additions into easy ones.

The "Friends of 10" Pairs:
1 + 9 = 10
2 + 8 = 10
3 + 7 = 10
4 + 6 = 10
5 + 5 = 10
Why This Works: Your brain loves round numbers (10, 20, 100). By borrowing a small amount from one number to "complete" the other to the nearest 10, you simplify the calculation.
Example: 8 + 7 becomes (8 + 2) + 5 = 10 + 5 = 15.

Worked Examples:

  1. 9 + 6: 9 needs 1 to become 10. Take 1 from 6 (leaving 5). Result: 10 + 5 = 15.
  2. 8 + 5: 8 needs 2 to become 10. Take 2 from 5 (leaving 3). Result: 10 + 3 = 13.
  3. 29 + 6: 29 needs 1 to become 30. Take 1 from 6 (leaving 5). Result: 30 + 5 = 35.
  4. 48 + 7: 48 needs 2 to become 50. Take 2 from 7 (leaving 5). Result: 50 + 5 = 55.
  5. 7 + 4: 7 needs 3 to become 10. Take 3 from 4 (leaving 1). Result: 10 + 1 = 11.
Try This!
What does 9 need to become 10? (Answer: 1)
Solve 19 + 5 using the complement method. (Answer: 24)
Common Mistake: Forgetting to subtract from the second number what you added to the first. If you add 2 to the 8, you MUST subtract 2 from the 7.

Real-World Application: You are buying a snack for $0.80 and have $0.50. How much is that? Think: 80 needs 20 to make 100 ($1.00). Take 20 from 50 (leaving 30). Total is $1.30.

1.2 Left-to-Right Addition

In school, you learned to add from right to left. But when you say numbers, you say them left to right (e.g., "four hundred..."). Vedic mathematics aligns calculation with speech. We add the biggest parts first!

Why This Works: It is faster because you get the most significant part of the answer immediately. It's also easier to hold in your working memory because you are building the number naturally.

Worked Examples:

  1. 46 + 38:
    • Step 1: 40 + 30 = 70
    • Step 2: 6 + 8 = 14
    • Step 3: 70 + 14 = 84
  2. 27 + 54: 20+50=70; 7+4=11; 70+11=81.
  3. 63 + 29: 60+20=80; 3+9=12; 80+12=92.
  4. 15 + 76: 10+70=80; 5+6=11; 80+11=91.
  5. 82 + 45: 80+40=120; 2+5=7; 120+7=127.
Try This!
Break 53 + 28 into two steps.
50 + 20 = ? (70)
3 + 8 = ? (11)
Total? (81)
Common Mistake: Forgetting the place value. In 46 + 38, the '4' is 40, not 4. Always say "forty" in your head, not "four".

1.3 Vertical Speed Addition

When you have 3-digit numbers or longer columns, vertical addition is efficient. But instead of carrying large numbers, we can use a "tick" or "dot" method to mark every time we reach 10.

Worked Examples:

  1. 345 + 267:
    • Hundreds: 3+2=5
    • Tens: 4+6=10 (write 0, carry 1 to hundreds) -> Hundreds becomes 6
    • Ones: 5+7=12 (write 2, carry 1 to tens) -> Tens becomes 1
    • Result: 612
  2. 589 + 123: 5+1=6; 8+2=10 (carry); 9+3=12 (carry). Result: 712.
  3. 147 + 258: 1+2=3; 4+5=9; 7+8=15 (carry). Result: 405.
  4. 672 + 184: 6+1=7; 7+8=15 (carry); 2+4=6. Result: 856.
  5. 923 + 145: 9+1=10; 2+4=6; 3+5=8. Result: 1068.

1.4 Mental Addition Chains

Building mental stamina is key. An addition chain is adding a series of single-digit numbers rapidly in your head. This trains your working memory.

Worked Examples:

  1. Chain: 5 + 6 + 3 + 7 + 2
    • 5+6=11
    • 11+3=14
    • 14+7=21
    • 21+2=23
  2. 4 + 8 + 6 + 2: 12, 18, 20.
  3. 9 + 5 + 5 + 1: 14, 19, 20. (Or see 5+5=10, 9+1=10, total 20!)
  4. 7 + 6 + 4 + 3: 13, 17, 20.
  5. 2 + 9 + 8 + 1: 11, 19, 20.

1.5 Multi-Number Addition (3-5 numbers)

Real life often involves adding more than two numbers (like a grocery list). The strategy is to look for chunks or pairs that are easy to add, rather than going strictly top-to-bottom.

Worked Examples:

  1. 24 + 36 + 15:
    • 24+36 = 60 (easy pair!)
    • 60+15 = 75
  2. 12 + 47 + 88: 12+88=100; 100+47=147.
  3. 35 + 25 + 18: 35+25=60; 60+18=78.
  4. 5 + 17 + 35 + 3: 17+3=20; 5+35=40; 20+40=60.
  5. 44 + 22 + 34: 44+34=78; 78+22=100.
